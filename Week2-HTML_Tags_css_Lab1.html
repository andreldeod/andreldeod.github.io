The Ocotillo


Fouquieria splendens

Ocotillo Wiki

Subspecies:
F. s. splendens Engelm
F. s. breviflora Hendrickson
F. s. campanulata (Nash) Henrickson

Alternate Common Names:
Candlewood, Coachwhip, Devil’s Walking Stick, Albarda, Ocotillo de Corral

Family:
Fouquieriaceae

Plant type:
woody shrub

Bloom period:
January-June, depending on temperature

Hardiness:
-10°F/-12°C





  1. Flower

    2. Ocotillo flowers are bright red . They bloom in the dry Spring of the Sonoran Desert on the borderlands of Sonora and Arizona. Red flowers tend to attract birds more than insect. Their blooming coincides with the northern migration of hummingbirds.








  2. Pollinator

    3. Ocotillo are thus a critical food source for various hummingbirds, especially migratory species like the Rufous hummingbird . Roufous humminbirds can migrate as far north as Alaska in the Summer and as far south as Jalisco in the Winter. When nectar availability is poor, the trip and time at refueling stops are prolonged. If there is no accessible food waiting at a stopover site, birds may die. Other hummingbirds that rely on ocotillos are Broad-billed,Violet-crowned, and the Costa's hummingbird. The hummingbird in the photo is a Costa's.








  3. Distribution

    4. Ocotillo's occur in the Sonoran, Chihuahuan, Colorado and Mojave deserts. In the US they grow in the southern Southwest. In Mexico they span from the Northwest to Central. They generally grow in dry rocky soils.
